Adjectives

Adjectives describe and modify nouns, not verbs, adverbs, or other adjectives. Adjectives tell us how much - or how many - of something someone is talking about. For example, My new dress is yellow and long. An adjective often comes before a noun: a short skirt, a peculiar man, an old building.

1 comparative adjectives

Comparative adjectives compare differences between 2 nouns. Learn English comparative adjectives usage and find out comparative adjectives types, examples and common mistakes
Adjectives with more syllables use 'more' + adjective + 'than.'
Some adjectives have irregular forms: 'good' -> 'better' and 'bad' -> 'worse.'

Example

  • My brother is taller than I am.
  • My daughter is older than my niece.
  • Her hair is longer than yours.
  • Your pizza looks better than mine.
  • This movie is more interesting than the last one.


    • 2 Compound adjectives

    Compound adjectives are multi-word adjectives that generally appear before a noun.

    Some compound adjectives can include more than 2 words.
    adjective-adjective noun
    adverb-adjective noun
    number-noun noun

    Example

  • He wore a blue-green shirt.
  • That looks like a well-fed cat.
  • The children get a 2-week break for the holidays.
  • He's undoubtedly a fast-talking salesperson.
  • She's a happy-go-lucky person.
  • It was an all-too-common complaint.

    • 3 Compound adjectives

    Demonstrative adjectives indicate specific things

    this/that + singular noun
    these/those + plural noun

    Example

  • I like that dress more.
  • I like this hat.
  • It will look nice with these shoes.
  • They like those clothes better.
  • That brown dog is his friend.

    • 4 descriptive adjectives

    Descriptive adjectives describe the size, color, or shape of a noun. They also describe nationality.
    adjective + [adjective +] noun

    Example

  • I love that big purple house.
  • The tiny dog and the large cat were best friends.
  • Yesterday was the hottest day of the year.
  • It is the most beautiful park in the city.

    • 5 Participle adjectives

    Present and past participles can be used as adjectives. An adjective that ends in -ing is used to describe: the characteristic of a person, a thing or a situation. An adjective that ends in -ed is used to describe: a feeling (or how a person feels) or an emotion

    present/past particIple + noun
    noun/pronoun + linking verb + present/past participle

    Example

  • Wesley thought the movie was slow and confusing.
  • Sam was fascinated by Mexico and its linguistic history.
  • I have never been more frustrated than when I was sitting in that traffic jam.
  • What a boring book! I've never been so bored in my life.

    • 6 possessive adjectives

    Possessive adjectives tell us, "this belongs to me/you/Peter/etc." A possessive adjective comes before the noun.
    possessive adjective + noun

    Example

  • My mother is American.
  • This is your book.
  • Is this your mother?
  • It's her birthday.
  • The dog eats in its food.

    • 7 superlative adjectives

    Adjectives describe nouns or pronouns. Superlative adjectives compare differences among 3 or more nouns.
    Adjectives with 1-2 syllables use '-est.' Adjectives with 3 or more syllables use 'the most/least.' Some adjectives have irregular forms.
    thing 1 + verb + adjective '-est' thing 1 + verb + 'the most/least' + adjective

    Example

  • Yesterday was the hottest day of the year.
  • It is the most beautiful park in the city.
  • It's the least crowded restaurant in the city.
  • That was the worst meal I ever ate.
